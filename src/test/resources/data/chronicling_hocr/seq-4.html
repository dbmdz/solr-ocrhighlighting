Moser) The velvet beans may be grown suc­ cesfully in neither section of the stale as a forage proposition blit not for seed is indicated by the results of the experiments of the Arkansas Ex­ periment Station as reported by L.W. Osborn, Agronomist. A considerable acreage of velvet beans was planted over the ontire state in 1919 but the crops was generally in failure since seed placed upon the market germin­ ated too poorly, that is, between 15 and 50 per cent and most of the seed was planted too eaily and failed be­ cause of a cold spring.

An experiment to determine correct dates for planting was conducted by the station in 1919, Results indicate that planting should not bn done un­ til the ground is thoroughly warm. At Fayetteville, all plantings which were made during April and until the first week in May were failures. The best stands were made from planters of May 15 and' 24, until July 1. The velvet bean is of tropical ori­ gin and makes its best growth in the real hot weather of July, August and September. It grows very slowly in cool weather, The cold, «ainy weath­ er which was general tin mghout the entire state in the spring of 1919 was particularly disastrous to velvet beans planted early. The speckled velvet bean which is the common variety grown, is said to mature seed ill the central cotton bolt and southward' in 120 days. By the magic power “Father Time.” | (impersonated by blie host), the years yore turned back and the guests were once more permitted to revel in in the joys of bygone days before the advent of the automobile, the motor heat and' the “tanga, tickle toe,” etc. Photos of the old style, and the good old songs served to bring back sweet memories, while the holiday greens, draped so gracefully over windows : nd doorway's bespoke the New Year Greeting of Prosperity and Good Cheer.
